Voiceover — Brendan Banfield double murder (Christine Banfield & Joseph Ryan)

## Chapter 1 (scenes 0001-0012)
Two bodies. A ransacked house in a quiet Virginia suburb. Police call it a home invasion gone wrong. But one of the victims isn't who this story needs him to be — and the husband standing in the driveway, telling officers he wasn't home when it happened, is about to become the reason none of it adds up.

The house belongs to Brendan and Christine Banfield, in Herndon, Fairfax County, Virginia. Inside, investigators find two bodies: Christine, a pediatric intensive care nurse, and a man named Joseph Ryan. The scene has been staged to read as a break-in — forced entry, disorder, the kind of chaos meant to tell a simple story. Except Joseph Ryan is not a burglar who wandered into the wrong house. And Brendan Banfield is not just a husband who came home to a nightmare.

Banfield spent his career as a federal law enforcement officer with the IRS — someone trained to build cases, not become the subject of one. As investigators pull the home invasion story apart, it stops holding together. Joseph Ryan wasn't there by accident. He came to that house because he believed he had a reason to be there.

Detectives trace Ryan's final movements back to a fetish website account — one he'd been messaging with, one he believed belonged to Christine Banfield. As far as Ryan knew, he was on his way to a consensual encounter with her. He was right that someone was waiting for him. He was wrong about who.

The account was never Christine's. Investigators determine it was controlled the entire time by Brendan Banfield and by Juliana Peres Magalhães, the Banfields' au pair — a young Brazilian woman living in the house to care for the couple's young daughter. Banfield was having an affair with her. Together, prosecutors allege, the two of them built a version of Christine convincing enough online to bring a real man to her front door.

## Chapter 2 (scenes 0013-0021)
This was never a break-in. It was bait. Joseph Ryan didn't die because he stumbled into a crime in progress — he died because he was deliberately lured into one, by two people who needed a stranger in that house to make Christine's murder look like something else entirely.

The case built toward a Fairfax County jury trial. Prosecutors laid out the affair, the fake account, and the staged scene as a single, deliberate plan — that Banfield and Magalhães conspired to frame Ryan as the intruder so Christine's murder would never be traced back to either of them.

On February 2nd, 2026, after nearly nine hours of deliberation across two days, the jury returned its verdict: guilty on all four counts — two counts of aggravated murder, one count of child endangerment, and one firearm charge. Magalhães never went to trial herself — she'd already pleaded guilty to manslaughter, testifying against Banfield instead.

Magalhães was sentenced to 10 years in prison. Banfield received the maximum the court could give him: life without the possibility of parole, handed down that June. The affair that started it, the fake account that lured Ryan to the house, the staged scene meant to bury it all — none of it held.

If the victim at a crime scene turns out to have been invited there on false pretenses — how much of what looks like a home invasion story is ever really what it looks like?
