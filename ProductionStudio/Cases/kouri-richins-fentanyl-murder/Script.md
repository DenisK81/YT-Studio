# Script.md — Kouri Richins fentanyl murder

A children's book about learning to live with grief. Written by the woman who caused it.

That's not a twist we're saving for the end. That's where this one starts, because nothing else about this case makes sense until you know that detail is real — and that it's not the strangest part of the story. Not even close.

Here's the setup. A husband. A wife. Three young sons. A house outside Park City, Utah — the kind of place people move to for the mountains and the quiet, not for anything resembling this. He runs a stonemasonry business. She flips houses for a living. On paper, it's a good life. The kind of life people photograph for a Christmas card.

Then, on a night in early March 2022, the husband is dead. Cause of death: fentanyl. Not a trace of it. Not an accidental brush with it. A dose roughly five times what it takes to kill a person.

And the wife tells police she doesn't understand it. He didn't do drugs. He never had. Not once, not that she knew of, not in all the years she'd known him.

So investigators are left holding a question that refuses to have an easy answer: how does a 39-year-old man with no history of drug abuse end up with five times a lethal dose of fentanyl in his system, in his own home, on an ordinary night, with no one else in the house claiming to have seen anything?

That question is the entire case. Everything that follows — every piece of evidence, every witness, every year this took to reach a courtroom — is the answer, assembled one piece at a time.

His name was Eric Richins. Hers is Kouri. And by the time a jury is finished with this story, the answer to that question will not be a tragic accident. It will be a plan. One that failed the first time, and did not fail the second.

Start with the night itself, because it matters how ordinary it looks from the outside. Eric drinks a Moscow Mule — nothing unusual, a drink he'd had plenty of times before, made more or less the way it's always made. Except this time, that drink is carrying enough fentanyl to kill several men his size. He goes to sleep. He does not wake up.

At first, without an obvious cause laid out in front of them, this reads like exactly what grief wants it to read like: a sudden, senseless tragedy. A healthy man, gone with no warning, no explanation, nothing anyone could have done. For a while, that's genuinely how the people around this family are allowed to understand it.

But investigators do something here that changes the entire shape of the case: they don't stop at "he had no history of drug use." They ask the harder, more uncomfortable question underneath it. If he wasn't using — if there is no plausible story where this drug enters his body by his own hand — then where did it come from? And who put it there?

The moment that question gets asked seriously, this case stops being a medical mystery and starts being a homicide investigation.

Because here is the first detail that refuses to fit a simple accident: this was not the first time.

About seventeen days before the night that finally killed him, prosecutors say, Eric was given a sandwich. It, too, was laced with fentanyl. He got sick. He did not die. Whoever did this — if this was deliberate, and investigators are now certain it was — tried once, failed, watched him survive, and came back seventeen days later to finish what the first attempt didn't.

Sit with that detail for a second, because it's the one that separates this from almost every other case like it. This isn't a single moment of rage or panic. This is someone who fails, recalculates, waits over two weeks, and tries again. That takes a kind of patience that should scare you more than the poison itself.

So who would do that to him? Investigators start where cases like this almost always start: close to home. And the deeper they look at Kouri Richins, the more the comfortable version of this family's life starts to come apart.

Start with the money, because money is where a lot of these stories quietly begin. The couple's real estate business is not the success story it looks like from the outside — not even close. Behind the surface, there's real estate debt reaching into the millions. A business that looks solid at a dinner party and is actually bleeding out behind the scenes, while the family keeps up every appearance of doing just fine.

Now stack insurance on top of that debt. Eric's life is covered for more than two million dollars, spread across multiple policies. And one of those policies — investigators find — was obtained using a forged signature. His forged signature. On a document he apparently never actually signed, for a policy he may never have known existed.

That's motive number one, laid out in plain financial terms: debt piling up on one side of the ledger, and a payout waiting on the other side of Eric's death.

Motive number two takes longer to surface, because it's personal, and people are good at hiding the personal ones. Kouri has been in a relationship with another man — Robert Josh Grossman — since early 2020. Two years, running quietly underneath the marriage, underneath the family photos, underneath all of it. And in messages investigators later recover, Kouri writes to him something that reads less like private longing and more like a plan already forming: "If he could just go away and you could just be here! Life would be so perfect!!"

Read that again, slowly. Not "I wish things were different." Not even "I wish I could leave him." "If he could just go away." That's not a woman imagining an escape. That's a woman imagining an ending.

So now you've got debt pulling in one direction and a relationship she wants to make permanent pulling in the other — and a husband standing directly between both of them.

From here, the evidence doesn't just accumulate. It compounds, each piece making the last one worse.

A former housekeeper, Carmen Lauber, comes forward with something investigators can't ignore. She says Kouri approached her — not once, but four separate times — asking for help getting something stronger than what she already had access to. At one point, according to her testimony, Kouri specifically asks for what she calls "the Michael Jackson stuff." That's not a vague, desperate ask. That's someone who knows exactly what she's requesting, and why.

Then there's Kouri's phone, and phones have a way of remembering things people wish they hadn't searched. Investigators find search history that includes "can cops force you to do a lie detector test" and "can deleted text messages be retrieved." Those aren't the searches of a grieving widow trying to make sense of a senseless death. Those are the searches of someone already calculating what she can hide, how thoroughly, and how fast — weeks and months before anyone has formally accused her of anything at all.

Every strand of evidence points the same direction, and none of them stand alone. There's the financial motive. There's the personal motive. There's a documented pattern of trying to acquire the exact drug that eventually killed him. There's a first attempt that failed, followed by a second that didn't. And running underneath all of it, there's a pattern of quiet, calculated panic about being caught — panic that predates any accusation.

And then comes the detail that turns this from a hard case into one people will be talking about for years.

About a year after Eric's death — while she is already a suspect, while investigators are quietly building the case that will eventually convict her, but before she has been formally charged with anything — Kouri Richins sits down and writes a children's picture book. It's called "Are You With Me?" It's about a child learning to process grief after losing a parent. She self-publishes it. She promotes it, on local media, in her own community. She says, in her own words, that she just wanted something to read to her own kids at night.

Sit with that for a moment, because it deserves more than a passing mention. A book meant to help a child process the loss of a parent — written, according to the very prosecutors building a murder case against her, by the parent who caused that loss in the first place. Promoted publicly. Sold to other families. All of it happening months before her arrest, while the investigation into her was already quietly underway.

Is that grief, channeled into something productive by someone who doesn't yet know she'll be caught? Is that a genuine attempt to process what she'd done, wrapped in denial? Or is it something colder — a performance of innocent, devoted motherhood, so convincing she put it into print and sold copies of it? The book itself doesn't answer that question. It just makes the question impossible to look away from.

A few weeks after that book reaches the public, in May of 2023, Kouri Richins is charged with murder.

What follows is not a quick trial, and it's worth saying that plainly, because real cases rarely move at the speed true-crime stories want them to. This one stretches on for years — delayed by disputes over evidence, by fights over legal representation, by jury selection that drags out far longer than anyone watching from the outside would expect. That gap, between charge and courtroom, is its own kind of quiet tension: years where the public accusation exists, but no verdict does.

Eventually, in early 2026, the case finally goes in front of a jury. And the prosecution builds its case methodically, witness by witness, piece by piece.

The housekeeper testifies again, this time under oath in front of the jury, walking through those four separate requests for stronger drugs. A forensic pathologist takes the stand and walks the jury through the toxicology in plain terms: fentanyl, at levels high enough to rule out any accidental explanation, any question of a dosing mistake, any benefit of the doubt.

And on March 4th, 2026 — almost exactly four years, to the week, after Eric's death — Robert Josh Grossman takes the stand himself. He testifies about the relationship. About the messages. About a future the two of them had apparently discussed, in a life where Eric simply wasn't in the way anymore.

Kouri's defense makes a choice that speaks for itself, in a courtroom where silence is its own kind of statement: she does not testify. Her legal team calls no witnesses at all. On March 13th, they rest their case without presenting a single one.

Closing arguments begin March 16th. Her defense asks for a mistrial, arguing the prosecution's closing remarks crossed a line. The judge disagrees, and rules the comments were fair.

Then the jury goes back to deliberate on a case that took four years to reach this room.

They return in three hours.

Guilty. On all five counts. First-degree aggravated murder. Attempted aggravated murder — for the sandwich, seventeen days before the drink that finished what it started. Two counts of insurance fraud. Forgery.

Three hours, for a case built over four years of investigation and delay. That's not a jury wrestling with reasonable doubt. That's a jury that heard the evidence laid out in full and didn't need long at all to agree on exactly what it meant.

The sentencing comes two months later, on May 13th, 2026 — a date that lands, by no design of the court's, on what would have been Eric's birthday.

Judge Richard Mrazik does not soften a single word of it. He describes what happened as calculated from the start — a woman who failed once, and then, in his words, spent the next seventeen days "doubling down, preparing to try again." He tells her directly that she is "simply too dangerous to ever be free."

The sentence: life without the possibility of parole for the murder itself. On top of that, five years to life for the attempted murder by sandwich. One to fifteen years on each of the two insurance fraud counts. Up to five more years for the forgery. All of it stacked to run consecutively, one sentence beginning only after the last one technically could have ended.

Eric's family speaks after the sentence is handed down. His sister Katie says that before his death, Eric had already come to believe Kouri was "evil" — his own word, according to his sister, used about the woman he was married to. Another sister, Amy, keeps her statement simple: "Just very happy that we got justice for my brother."

And Kouri — even now, even convicted on all five counts, even sentenced to die in prison — does not admit to any of it. She tells the court: "God did not put me in this world to take a life." She says her contact with her own three sons has been blocked for more than two years. She says she intends to appeal, and the court grants her extra time — twenty-eight days instead of the standard fourteen — to file it.

Whether that appeal changes anything is still an open question, one that hasn't been answered as of this telling. What isn't in question, according to twelve jurors who heard every piece of this evidence, is what happened inside that house in March of 2022 — and why it happened twice before it finally worked.

So here's what this case actually leaves behind. A woman who tried once, failed, waited seventeen days, and tried again. Who searched for ways to cover her tracks before a single person had accused her of anything. And who, while investigators were quietly assembling the case that would eventually convict her, sat down and wrote a book to help other families' children process exactly the kind of loss she stands convicted of causing.
